Xi’an

I took a soft sleeper from Beijing to Xi’an, sharing with thee Chinese who spoke 200 words of English between them. Thanks to my maps, travel guide, some dumplings and a decent portion of patience, we managed to get by quite fine. The sleeper train was excellent, arriving in Xi’an the next morning, fully refreshed (the ride took 12 hours).

On the train, I also met two really nice Polish girls who knew Xi’an from before. This was excellent as they knew their way around the city and how to get to the famous Terracotta Warriors.

The Green Men

I was only moderately enthusiastic about seeing the Terracotta Army, a bunch of statues I was thinking. But, I must say the Terracotta Warriors is one of the most impressive historic sights I’ve ever seen. In my book, this was up there with the fantastic Louvre in Paris. When you think of the size of the army (they’ve only excavated a fraction of it), the time they where made, the fact that each solider is different from the previous one, that the army was staged under ground, never to be discovered, that all of them had real weapons so that they could defend the buried emperor in the “after life”.

One of the reasons why they haven’t excavated the rest of the army, is that they don’t know how to preserve the colours. All of the warriors were painted in bright colours, but these colours fade the moment they’re exposed to normal air and light. So, until the technology is good enough to preserve them, they’re keeping the rest of the army underground

The Old Town

Xi’an soon became my favourite stay on my China trip. I hadn’t been in the city for more than 20 minutes and had already been helped by two sets of Chinese who just asked if I needed help. Having just been in Beijing, I was taken by surprise of this helpfulness (I must admit I was a bit rude to the first inquiry expecting whatnot after five days in Beijing). And this continued through my stay in Xi’an, people you meet in the streets are on the whole are friendly and helpful.

As you disembark the train, the first impressive view presents itself; the city wall. A 600 year wall from the Quing dynasty goes around the old town. It’s 100% intact and you can walk or bike it all around. I recommend going there after sunset as it gives a spectacular view of the city at night and the guard houses and walls are beautifully lit.

Maybe the most colourful spot in the old town, is the Muslim Quarter. A myriad of small alleys and narrow streets, a bustling market with crafts and foods from both the Chinese and Muslim society. It comes highly recommendable (you must try the soup with bread crumbs in it, as well as the rice mixed with plum on a stick!), I only hope the Muslim minority stays well represented at the market…

Sweet rice on a stick with plum thing - delecious!

Inside the Muslim Quarter you’ll also find the Great Mosque of Xi’an, the biggest mosque in China. It’s a good place with many buildings, beautiful parks and patios. The architecture is hugely interesting with a great mix of Arabic and Chinese styles.

After my Polish travelling companions had left the next day, I met a really sweet girl from Shanghai with whom I discovered more of Xi’an. Needlessly to say, having someone with me who spoke Chinese was a real treat

The city has two pagodas that are worth a visit; The Big Goose Pagoda and The Little Goose Pagoda. The former is a Buddhist monastery that’s really nice and settling to walk around.with patios, beautiful stone statues and green parks . The latter is connected to a really museum, namely The Shaanxi History Museum. It’s definitely among the better museums and the audio guide was excellent (but be sure to get one that’s working!).

I truly enjoyed the street food in Xi’an, especially the spicy, thick pancake like thing. The buns () were huge and came in several different flavours. The breakfast got even better when taking it into the Geming Park (西安市革命公园) where we could watch the locals (mostly old people) doing their morning aerobic, Tai Chi, badminton and dance (!).

When talking about food, there is one place you go in Xi’an; De Fa Chang Jiao Zi Guan (德发长饺子馆). It’s a dumpling restaurant with only eight dishes on the menu, seven of which are dumplings (boiled and steamed). It was a definite gastronomical highlight of the trip. So good, I ate there three times …

Another place I’d like to recommend, is a Japanese restaurant Roppongi; it’s extremely clean, fresh, elegant and truly delicious food - a great variation away from most Chinese foods. It’s close to McDonald’s near the south side of the Bell Tower.
