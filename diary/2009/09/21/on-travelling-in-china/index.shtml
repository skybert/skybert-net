On Travelling in China

Hands

Your hands are dirty. Let me say that again; your hands are dirty. Try to keep them clean, yes, but always assume they’re dirty. As a rule of thumb in this part of Asia, people’s left hand is dirtier than the right, but after Western standards, they’re both dirty.

This means, never eat with your hands. If eating a cookie, use a napkin around, use the plastic wrapping etc. When eating dumplings from your favourite baozi stand on the corner, use the plastic bag the dumplings come in to hold the bun you’re eating.

In the toilet, use some paper to lift the seat, or use your foot to kick it open (gently does it).

Water

Always have water in hand. I like to always have some in reserve in case I can’t get another bottle at the very second I want/need it. Having a big bottle (da ping shui) or two normal ones (lian ping shui) makes sense.

Food

Food’s defintely on my China top three list. And eating from stalls in the street and in 3m2 big restaurants are the best places to do so. Always seek out places that are busy, where locals go. If there’s tons of people, it means that the food is made fresh. If it’s made fresh, it’s safe to eat as all bacteria die at 70C. Which leads me to my next rule of thumb, always eat hot food …

Probably the most dangerous places to eat are slow tourist restaurants. First of all, the food is not made fresh, but just as important; there’s few/no locals eating there; the restaurant doesn’t need satisfied customers to stay alive as all the visitors are one/two time guests anyway.

On the contrary, the old woman in the street that makes banana pancakes from her wee stall has done this 10 hours a day for the last 20 years. She makes this one dish and makes it well. That’s why locals buy from her every morning at six on their way to work. And this is why you should feel (pretty) safe eating her food :-)

Ripping you off

Just get used to it, if you’re not local, they’ll try to over charge you when buying food or goods at the street or in shops. The degree to which this happens varies from person to person and from city to city, but as a rule of thumb, expect to be over charged (and bargain thereafter).

As far as I could tell, though, restaurants are a nice exception to this. Also, street food sales people charging Y1-2 are accurate enough, as is water bottles for Y1-2

When taking a Taxi, insist that he/she’s using the meter. If travelling longer distances (e.g. the airport 45 mins away) agree on a price on the beforehand (duoshao qian). Otherwise, they’ll often try to rip you off (e.g. my driver in Jinghong tried to get Y50 off me when driving the 5km out to the airport, where he should (as far as I could understand) have asked for Y10-20).

When all this is said, I want to add that that I’ve only experience that they want to fool you about monetary issues. They don’t take the piss sending you to the wrong direction, on the wrong bus etc. It’s just in money matters you need to take more heed than normal.

Trust your sense of direction

Get yourself a good map (local book shop, Lonely Planet, tourist information etc) and find what’s north: the sun rises in east and goes down in west. At noon, the sun is in the south. That’s the basics.

Know this; although Chinese people are a friendly, helpful bunch, they’re in my experience, really bad in reading maps. I don’t know why this is, but don’t be surprised if a taxi driver or bus chauffeur is not able to tell you where in their city you are on the map (even when the map’s in perfect Chinese).

Also, if you believe you should be going left and the bus driver argues right, big chances are you’re the one that’s right Again, not based on academic research, only my own experience from three weeks of travelling.

Diarrhoea

It’s perfectly normal to get diarrhoea when travelling in the East. Don’t panic! There’s many degrees of diarrhoea, I managed to get through the night by just keeping it in, but if you shit like piss, these are things that should help (I’ve gathered this form an avid traveller, a doctor and a guide book):

  • Drink flat Coke (no water till your shit is in order again).
  • Drink soya sauce (yupp, straight from the bottle, we want the body to get salt so that the food “sticks” again.
  • bananas are good, but if your diarrhoea is bad, don’t eat for 24 hours
  • Get a hold of Specta, I’ve uploaded an image of it here with Chinese letters, show it in the pharmacy as they probably don’t understand English. Spectra is not strong, so you can take it several times a day.

Trust your gut feeling

If a guy seems sleezy, great chances are that he is. If your gut says you shouldn’t enter a restaurant, don’t do it. Trusting your gut feeling makes travelling in general a lot easier, I’ve found it. Less thinking, more feeling and hopefully rewarding experiences

First aid kit: maps, notepad & dictionary

I only spoke a handful of sentences in Chinese, but got by quite well by using my dictionary that both I and the Chinese could look up words in, a map and a notepad for scribbling - or more importantly, jotting down small drawings in. A wee sketch can communicate many words …
