Ruili

p>In Kunming airport, I got to know this very nice Burmanese couple who also
was traveling to Ruili this evening. Hence, we shared a taxi (and food) together
and they helped me finding a hotel and bargained the price down from Y260 to Y100.
What wonderful people you meet when you travel to strange places!

Entrance to one of Ruili's markets

Walking around Ruili was an interesting experience indeed. While traveling in China for two weeks, I had gotten used being “The Foreigner” and everything being unfamiliar, unreadable, not understandable and so on, but Ruili took this one further. I’m pretty sure I was the only white guy in town, not a single sign was in English, not a single menu was and hardly a living soul spoke a word of English. This was great! I loved it, walking around the streets, talking to people in my very limited Chinese. You know there’s something special going on when I can speek more Mandarin than they can speak English

The city was laid back and ok enough to walk around - and a shopping haven for jade and wood artifacts. Since I’m not into any of that, I was quite happy leaving town the next day and join a two hour mini bus ride to Mangshi.

The bus stop had of course changed since the guide book was written and I found it thanks to three sweet wee girls who walked with me for an hour (!) to locate it and the correct bus. Again, I was taken by the helpfulness of the Chinese people.

Sign on bus from Ruili to Mangshi

The ride itself was a great experience with eight people sitting on the floor in the bus, carrying with them everything from barrels of food to construction materials. On the way, the bus was stopped by several policemen who checked the ID papers of everyone and went through the luggage. All understandable as Ruili is said to be the opium gate in south China, from which the drugs go to Hong Kong.
