Enjoying Bangladesh


 Old Dhaka

Sunday, I returned from two joyful weeks in Bangladesh. The prime reason for going down there was to visit the Escenic Asia ofice in Dhaka. After working with the Dhaka office since its inception in Janurary 2007, I've come to know a lot of Bangladeshi colleagues. Some have come to Norway on the Norwegian Peace Corp exchange programme and these fine people I've gotten to know really well. However, the office is so much bigger, counting around 50 folks, and therefore the main incentive for the trip was to get to know everyone better, spread some good faith and make sure my Dhaka team works as well as possible with their Oslo counterparts.

So, the days in the office flew away, normally commencing at six and seven in the evening without me having had a chance to look at the watch. After that, the jolly good lads and lassies in the office (not everyone at once, of course) joined me for an adventure into the jungle of restaurants in Dhaka.

What a food feast it was! A myrriad of curries, nan bread, biryani, chicken, beef & mutton. I tried most things they put in front of me (only green mustard fish curry was too spicy for me, the stomach reacted and I started getting the hick ups!) and I can report that "yes, kidneys taste ok".

Thanks to our excellent driver, I got to discover many parts of Dhaka in as little time as the omnipresent traffic jam admitted, as well as various villages and markets in the outer Dhaka Division area.

During my stay in Bangladesh, it just so happened that one of my colleagues got married and I was fortunate to get invited to their wedding. It was the first Bangladeshi wedding and a complete new experience to me! I came well prepared, bringing my punjabi with me from Norway, still I somehow didn't blend quite in ... Mhmm, maybe because of 1) being two heads taller than most people 2) not speeking Bangla and 3) being white. Oh well, the wedding was good fun anyhow. Many people from the office were there and we had all a great time, enjoying the spectacle, good food and even watched The Netherlands beat Brazil!.

So what about the traffic jam? Yes, what about it? It's really funny, the first questions everyone asked me was: 1) Where do you come from? and 2) What do you think of the traffic jam?

And to be frank, I didn't mind it so much. Yes, the traffic chaos is omnipresent, yes, they are honking the horns all the time, yes there are countless poor people knocking on your windows wanting money or wanting to sell you something, yes it takes time to travel from A to B, but having such a great driver, riding in a nice air conditioned car (it seems that 90% of the cars are white Toyota Corollas), it's just like riding a boat: you cannot do anything about the time, so just relax. Of course, if I were living in Dhaka for five years rather than two weeks, I might have gotten annoyed about it, but for two weeks, it didn't get to me :-)

The time also allowed for a wee excursion with some of the guys to the Padma river. It was a beautiul day with sun shining from a clear sky. We enjoyed wee villages, a river resort, good food and, in my book, most of all: Riding on the river in a (what I would call) fishing boat.

I was also fortunate enough to get invited for tea at one colleague's place and dinner with a former colleague's family. It's really exciting to see how people live and I'm very thankful that they wanted to open up their homes for me.

During my two weeks, I went jogging a couple of times, something which was a strange experience. The driver brought me to a couple of parks where several people were doing their morning exercise, albeit this mostly consisted of walking, not jogging. Hence, they looked at me for two reasons: Being the only foreigner in the park, and often being the only one jogging (what is he thinking, it's hard enough to walk in this humidity!) And, I can tell you, I felt like I was suffocating in the imaginary wall of heat and humidity, forcing me to keep a slow pace. Still, it was great exercise leaving me soaked in sweat and hungry for breakfast at the end of it!

All in all, it was a great two weeks, introducing me to a new country ar away from home - in more than one sense of the word :-)
