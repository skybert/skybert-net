Loving My Desiré

I've finally followed suit and gotten myself a so called "smart phone". It'll be interesting to see if it will be "smart" or just "try to be helpful" but end up being a pain in the butt, such as the infamous Office assistant (The Paper Clip Man in Word).

The phone I ended up with was HTC Desire, mobile phone based on Google's Android platform, which again builds on the Linux kernel and the Java programming platform.

First the Things People Like Me Care About (and no normal person would even consider to spend a millisecond thinking about)

From a technical point of view, it was an easy choice (and indeed an unsurprising one for anyone that knows me); it runs the OS that I've been using as my desktop OS for the last ten years and the applications are built using my favourite programming language.

To top it all off, it can play OGG Vorbis music files. This means that I finally have a device that can play my entire music collection, instead of just the 30% that is using patended formats such as MP3.

And Then What the Normal People Care About

So, what other things (like what matters to most people) do I like about this phone - except its extremey cheesy name?

While we're tlaking about the increadibly cheesy name, I prefer referring to my phone as Desiré instead of Desire, it somehow sounds better :-)

Syncronisation Works for Everyone

A wonderful side effect that the makers of the phone do not have their own OS (well, I do know about Chrome OS, but it's currently not a player at all), is that it syncs wonderfully well with all kinds of platforms. It is admittedly, and not surprisingly, tightly coupled with your Google account, using it for calendar, mail and contacts.

Be that as it may, the HTC Desire syncs perfectly well with Linux, Windows and Mac. Completely without installing additional software of any kind, I could instantly sync my calendars (I've got 5-8 diffferent ones I use every day), mail and file contents. Transfering files was only a matter of plugging in the USB cable and drag and drop files from my file manager.

A Person Is a Person

On of the things I like the best with the Desire, is that it treats a person a person. Ok, that sounds stupid. Let me explain; it uses all the information you give it, such as your Google account, your Facebook account, your Flickr account and your Twitter account. Then it aggregates all of these contacts and lets you (and sometimes it figures it out all by itself), that your Google contact "Lisa Jefferson", your SIM card contact "Lisa Jefferson" and your Facebook contact "Lisa Jefferson" are all, in fact the same person and thus deserves to be included on the same entry in the address book. When you look at Lisa in your address book, Desire will give you the choices how you want to communicate with your friend, be that phone, text message or email. It's really cleverly done and it's one of the surprising features of the phone.

Browsing is A-mazing

The browser that ships with the phone is amazingly good, making excellent use of the phone capabilities, changing view depending on how you hold the phone, zooming in and out using your fingers, sliding text to the side as you lead your fingers over the screen and so on.

Typing Messages Actually Works

One of the things I was really sceptical about, was typing text messages, not to mention emails on a screen based keyboard. How can this possible work when you don't feel the keys?

After using the phone for just one week, I must say it's surprisingly good at typing mesages. It is not as good as normal mobile phone, and I believe it will never be as you cannot feel the keys and thus are dependent on looking at the screen the whole time. Apart from that, it's miraculously good at guess what I'm trying to type, and even suggests the correct words when I've typed the wrong keys (!). Hence, it understand the context/location of the the mistyped characters and makes very well educated guesses on what I was actually trying to type!

YouTube

Watching YouTube films works great, it even has a dedicated YouTube application that it suggests using whenever encountering a YouTube link in the browser.

And more ...

I'm sure I'll discover more gems as I use the phone more. Getting shell access to the phone is definitely on my list on things to try out. But for now, I'm happy just browsing, tipping, panning, snapping, reading - and not to to forget calling using my new toy.
