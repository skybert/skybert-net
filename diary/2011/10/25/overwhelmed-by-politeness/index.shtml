overwhelmed by politeness

I'm overwhelmed by politeness. I have never in my life experience anything like this. People everywhere I turned were utterly and completely polite. As you might have guessed by reading this far, I've just gotten back from four days in the wonderful city of Tokyo, Japan.

This was my first visit to the country of Japan, a country I've through the years read so much about. From I started training karate at the age of eight, Japan has always been a symbol of honour, perfection and effectiveness. Visiting the country for the first time, I will be quick to add on top of this list: politeness.

During my four days in Tokyo, I found people to be first of all polite, then secondary; friendly. Everyone bows. Everyone smiles. The presence of the face saving aspect that I have found so many places in the East has never been more prevalent than in Japan. Everyone is set to avoid embarrassing moments. For themselves, but also for their fellow country men or indeed visitors.

One consequence of this, is that everyone talks to you in Japanese! I constantly found myself under a waterfall of Japanese, smiles and last but not least: politeness. Whoever I turned to, I was treated as if I were fluent in Japanese, something which couldn't be further from the truth. I could say thank you and count to five. That was just about it.

The absence of English proficiency, even among young people proved to be of no problem, due to everyone's insistence on being of service and wanting it to work. However, one evening there was an absolute lack of understanding. The 20-something girl, sweet and friendly as she was, just did not understand what we wanted. When all else failed, pointing at pictures included, I made a shot in the dark: Nǐ shuō zhōngwen ma? And guess what: she did speak Mandarin. And as a spell was broken, the river of communication could flow again :-)

All in all, Japan was a refreshing experience and a good get away from Taiwan. It's very different from Taiwan. It's a bit more like the West. It's quieter. Much quieter. The omnipresent noise that you find in Taiwan, is not there. The millions of Tokyo-ians, manage to get by perfectly by making no more noise than an average afternoon in my beloved Sarpsborg in the south of Norway. Glorious!

Seeing the tidiness, the effectiveness and subtleness of the entire city, the buildings, the restaurants, the shots was very interesting indeed and it made for a perfect backdrop for a good time with my friends studying at the university there.

One day I'll be back, but probably then exploring other parts of this wonderful country. I'm especially curious of Mount Fuji and the mountainous North. We'll see. For now, I'll just say: あなたの日本に感謝!
