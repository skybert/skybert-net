The Day Norway Lost Her Innocence

Nothing written, pictured or shown on TV touched my heart deeper and unleashed the intense grief of my heart than what the Swedish foreign minister, Carl Bildt, wrote:

Terrorism has struck. Police confirms bomb in Oslo. We are all Norwegians.

One hour after the bomb struck, I got the first email from a friend in Norway telling me that a terrorist bomb had struck 500 metres away from my home in Oslo. The following minutes and hours, the even more horrifying news of the Utt√∏ya massacre came through Twitter, Facebook, Email and SMSs to me in Taiwan.

As I biked home that evening (I'm six hours ahead of Norway), my sadness was of course over the victims of this lunatic, but even more so over my country. I felt this was something that we just didn't think would happen in our beloved Norway. A deep and heavy sadness stuck in my gut: the Norway I loved and cherished would never be the same after this. On this day, Norway lost her innocence.

After I had described my feelings to the friends around me here in Taiwan, I came over this BBC article which sums up a lot of what I feel about this terrible day. On this day, Norway lost her innocence.

In the midst of all the sorrow, there's been beauty: I've received heart warming greetings from friends in Norway, Scotland, Germany, Italy, Taiwan, Australia and Saudi Arabia. Also, people I even don't even know the name of, in my daily life that know I'm Norwegian have come up to me to give me their support. It's immensely good to feel friends' and strangers' support in times like these. Thank you so much.
